How RMDs work: The IRS requires withdrawals from tax-deferred retirement accounts starting at age 70½ (born 1950 or earlier), 73 (born 1951–1959), or 75 (born 1960 or later) per SECURE 2.0. Your RMD = prior year-end balance ÷ IRS life expectancy factor. This calculator uses the Uniform Lifetime Table, which applies to most IRA owners.

Ways to lower your RMDs
  • → Convert to a Roth IRA — Roth IRAs have no RMDs for the original owner. Converting traditional IRA funds reduces future RMD amounts, though you'll owe income tax on the converted amount. Converting smaller amounts over several years can spread the tax burden.
  • → Donate via Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) — IRA owners age 70½ or older can transfer up to $111,000 directly to charity each year (2026 limit, indexed for inflation). The QCD counts toward your RMD but does not appear in adjusted gross income — a benefit even for those who take the standard deduction.
  • → Still working? Use the work waiver — If you're employed past RMD age and own less than 5% of the company, you can defer RMDs from your current employer's 401(k) or 403(b) until you actually retire.
  • → Use a Qualified Longevity Annuity Contract (QLAC) — You can invest up to $210,000 (2026 limit, indexed for inflation) in a QLAC. That portion is excluded from your RMD calculation, deferring payments until around age 85.
  • → Younger spouse rule — If your spouse is more than 10 years younger and is the sole primary beneficiary of your IRA, you can use the IRS Joint Life and Last Survivor Table instead of the Uniform Lifetime Table, which produces a larger divisor and a smaller required withdrawal each year.
  • → Nondeductible contributions reduce your tax bill — If you made after-tax contributions to a traditional IRA, a proportional share of each distribution is tax-free. Keep records of these contributions to calculate the tax-free portion each year.
